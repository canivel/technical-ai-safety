What do future do you want?

To tackle a problem, it's important to understand what you're working toward. This exercise aims to clarify your vision for AI's role in the world. 

Throughout the course, you'll evaluating different approaches to making AI safer which require you to figure out what future they are bringing us closer to. This exercise will help you ground those answers.

You might want to consider:

In 10 years, what can AI do? Who controls it? Who is steering its development?
What risks are you most worried about? How are we defending against them?
What benefits are most important to protect?
What would make you say "we succeeded" versus "we failed"?

In 10 years:

AI systems that are genuinely transformative (in the TAI sense from the readings) — capable of accelerating scientific research, improving healthcare diagnostics, and augmenting human creativity. However, I want these systems to remain tools that amplify human agency rather than autonomous agents pursuing their own goals.

Who controls it: A distributed ecosystem — not concentrated in a few frontier labs, but with meaningful oversight from democratic institutions, independent safety researchers, and civil society. The "Build it slowly and safely" approach resonates most with me: treating AI development more like nuclear energy or pharmaceuticals with enforced safety standards.

Who steers development: I want alignment researchers and safety teams to have genuine veto power over deployment decisions, not just advisory roles that get overruled by commercial pressures.

Risks I'm most worried about





Goal misgeneralization at scale — The Claude/Stockfish examples in the readings are chilling. As systems become more capable, the gap between what we specify and what they learn to pursue could have catastrophic consequences.



The coordination failure — Everyone building slowly while "someone else" races ahead. The game-theoretic pressure toward "Accept the race" even when all parties would prefer controlled development.



Emergent capabilities we don't understand — Per the readings: "Models develop capabilities we never trained them for. They exhibit behaviours we can't explain."

Benefits most important to protect





AI-assisted scientific discovery (especially in medicine and climate)



Democratized access to expertise (education, legal aid, healthcare in underserved areas)



Human autonomy and meaningful work — AI should expand what humans can do, not replace human judgment entirely

Success vs. Failure

We succeeded if: In 2035, AI systems are demonstrably more capable than today, and we have robust interpretability tools that let us understand why they make decisions, and there have been no catastrophic alignment failures, and the benefits are broadly distributed rather than captured by a small elite.

We failed if: We either (a) experience an existential catastrophe from misaligned AI, (b) create a permanent power imbalance where AI control is concentrated in ways that undermine democratic governance, or (c) trigger a race-to-the-bottom on safety that normalizes cutting corners.

The inner/outer alignment distinction from Shah's piece is instructive here — even defining "success" is hard when we can't cleanly categorize failures. But I'd rather aim for a fuzzy good future than a precisely specified bad one.
