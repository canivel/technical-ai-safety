You're at the beginning of your technical AI safety journey.

This course gave you a structured overview of technical AI safety: the key challenges behind making AI safer, the current safety techniques, and their gaps. 

You’ve started developing some research taste — the ability to assess which areas of technical AI safety are impactful to work on and why. With <2,000 people working full-time on making AI systems safer (based on our estimates), the field needs people with high agency – those willing to take bold action even when it’s hard.

There’s no clear roadmap, instructions or prophets to tell us what we should do – just people who care deeply, doing what they believe will steer us toward a positive future. You don't need a perfect inside view on AI safety to start. This will develop as you experiment. In the meantime, lean on open problems and proposals from organisations you respect.

Many have written great advice on technical AI safety careers, like 80,000 Hours, Marius Hobbhahn (CEO and Founder of Apollo), Neel Nanda (leading mech interp researcher at Google DeepMind). This synthesises that advice into specific actions you can take after this course.

The most common advice we give to people is:

1: When in doubt, give it a shot!

If you’re not sure whether you’re the right fit for a role (and it’s a relatively low-cost action), just apply. If you’re not sure whether you have the skills to do a particular project, time-bound yourself and try it.

(But be wary of doing hard-to-reverse things without careful thought, e.g. being very noisy in the governance space. Replicating papers and posting on your Substack is usually safe.)

2: Build in public

One of the best ways to get feedback, signal you’re serious and find collaborators is to tell other people about it! Your ideas will not do you much good sitting in a Google Doc.

Post on LinkedIn, start a Substack, go to AI safety events and talk to people about it. No one is expecting perfection.

3: Don’t wait for permission

Never assume someone else is solving the problem you care about.

Even if they are, extra hands are almost always welcome. There’s far more work than people, and they could always use someone with the right skills who’s keen to work on it.

4: Consider neglectedness. 

One of the key reasons why AI safety is so important for more people to work on is how few people work in it relative to its importance to the world. Similarly, within AI safety, there are areas and organisations that receive more attention, and others that receive less. 

While the better-trodden paths may have great career capital and be easiest to see, also consider where you have special insight or knowledge that fewer people are working on.

5: Do things that are galaxy-brain resistant.

Often it is very easy to rationalise actions (like joining a frontier lab pushing AI capabilities so that you can “be in the room where it happens”). But it can be valuable to take a step back, and consider whether you could use similar arguments to rationalise anything and generally pursue theories of change that are simple, robust, and do not have extremely high downside risk.

Most people might find it helpful to explore these broad areas:

Research
Implement techniques to make AI safer.

This could involve directly training models, cross-pollinating ideas from other fields or more theoretical work. For example, the concept of model organisms first appeared in biology, and the practice of red-teaming comes from cybersecurity.

Having a deep understanding of ML is incredibly valuable, but those with less ML experience can likely make up for it by being the best within your niche.

Founding
Start the initiatives that will make AI systems safer.

There may be orgs already working on the thing you care about that you can join, but sometimes headcount, culture fit, location, and other factors can get in the way. Sometimes, there isn’t anyone working on the particular thing you care about.

Having good ideas and being high agency is incredibly helpful. You can apply to our incubator week or other places like Seldon and 5050.

Though working on the models themselves is the most obvious way to contribute, it would be a mistake to think that is the ONLY way to make an impact. AI researchers don’t operate in isolation. They work alongside operations staff, designers, product managers, research managers, recruiters, and subject matter experts.

Many impactful roles exist beyond the categories above. Lean into your unique strengths.

Note: Technical work is not enough to ensure AI systems are safe. We also need:

Governance mechanisms that enforce safety protocols on all AI developers
AI security to prevent model theft or escape
Societal defenses for harms that slip through (e.g. biosecurity, cybersecurity)
